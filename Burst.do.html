Phoneme Identification With Chips

Goal: Given a spoken word, the student can say the first, middle, or last sound of the word.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Students place chips in front of them for each phoneme they hear in a spoken word and identify which chip represents the first, middle, and last sound they hear in the word.

3–5 chips per student.
Picture cards: bag, box, dice, elk, foot, game, moon, owl, run, tire, vet, wave.

Gather appropriate picture cards.

MODEL

1

Give each student three to five chips. Select the picture card foot.

2

We’re going to place one chip in front of us for each sound we say in a word. My turn first. My word is foot, fffooot. Place one chip in front of you as you say each phoneme until you have three chips in a row.

3

Now I want you to tell me what sound each chip stands for in the word. Point to the first chip. What sound does this chip stand for in foot? Students: /f/. Point to the middle chip. What sound does this chip stand for in foot? Students: /o/. Point to the last chip. What sound does this chip stand for in foot? Students: /t/.

PRACTICE

4

Select the picture card run.

5

Now it’s your turn. Pick up your chips. Place one chip down for each sound in this word: run, rrruuunnn. What sound does this chip stand for? Point to the first chip and call on a student to identify the correct phoneme. Repeat for the middle and last phonemes, calling on different students.

6

Okay, let’s try another. Pick up your chips. Now we’re going to use chips to stand for each sound in this word: wave. Continue the activity using the remaining picture cards. Be sure to call on students individually to check for understanding. Vary the order in which you ask for sounds; for example, ask for middle, last, and first.

7

For students who struggle, model putting down the chips and segmenting the word. Have the student repeat what you did and then try it on his or her own with a different word.

Have three students stand in a line, representing the first, middle, and last sound. When a struggling student points to a standing student, the standing student says the respective sound. Then have the struggling student identify each sound.

Give the students more chips and have them practice using longer words with four or five phonemes, such as best, plan, stretch, and panda.

Walk It Out: Phoneme Segmentation Accuracy

Goal: Given a spoken word, the student can segment it into separate sounds.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Students segment the phonemes in a given word and take one step for each phoneme in the word.

None.

Make a list of these words for you to refer to: box, bug, did, get, hill, moon, pen, sack, sing, time, top, van.

MODEL

1

Line up the students in a row.

Today we are going to count sounds in words. I am going to say a word, and you will count the number of sounds it has. Remember to count the sounds, not the syllables. You will take one step for each sound you hear.

2

My turn first. My word is moon, mmmooonnn. Take one step forward as you say each phoneme. How many sounds are in moon? Yes, there are three sounds in the word moon.

PRACTICE

3

Now it’s your turn. The next word is: hill. Call on an individual student to segment hill. Then continue with the list of words, calling on other students individually.

4

Watch for students who do not segment the word out loud or who do not step in time with the phonemes. Model for those students again and have them try it on their own.

Partner a struggling student with a stronger one and have them stand side by side and link elbows. When you give them a word, have them take steps in unison for each phoneme so the struggling student can “feel” each phoneme.

Introduce words with consonant blends, such as stretch and play. Students will need to take extra steps for the additional phonemes.

Introduce v

Goal: Given a printed letter, the student can say its sound.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Each student says /v/ when the teacher points to v, and gives a thumbs-down and remains silent when the teacher points to other letters.

Board to write on.

Write a large letter v on the board.

MODEL

1

Point to the letter v.

Today, we are going to learn the sound this letter makes. The sound for this letter is /vvv/. Say the /v/ sound as in vet, holding it for at least a second.

2

When you say /vvv/, you put your top teeth on your bottom lip and gently blow with your voice box on: /vvv/. What’s the sound? Students: /vvv/.

3

Give each student an individual turn to say /vvv/. Model it again for any student who is saying the wrong sound.

4

We can hear the /v/ at the start of the words very and voice.

5

Erase v. Now write 12 letters on the board, arranging them randomly. Make four of the items v and intersperse them with eight other letters dissimilar in appearance to v.

When I point to the letter we just learned, say its sound. When I point to any other letter, you will give me a thumbs-down and stay quiet. My turn first.

6

Point to a series of letters and model either saying the sound or emphasizing a thumbs-down motion, as appropriate.

PRACTICE

7

Now it’s your turn. Ready?

8

After several group responses, call on individual students to identify sounds.

For students who say something for all letters or letter combinations, have them cover their mouths on the items that are not v. For students who do not pronounce the sound correctly, give them a small mirror. Model the sound for them, and have them practice saying the sound while watching their mouths in the mirror.

Have the students say both /v/ and a word that contains that sound each time you point to the target letter. You can also ask students to put the word in a sentence.

Blast Off: Letter Sound Fluency

Goal: Given a printed letter, the student can say its sound with automaticity.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Each student says the letter sounds for a vertical list of letters, saying “Blast off!” after the last letter.

Watch with a second hand or timer.
  • Board to write on.

Write the letters vertically on the board in a random order so that there are 10 letters total. Use the following letters: a, m, s, t, i, f, r, o, d, u, g, c, b, n, k. (You can repeat letters, focus only on letters that students struggle with, and omit letters you think are too easy.)

MODEL

1

These letters are written in a column like a rocket on a launch pad. Instead of counting numbers down to blast off, we are going to say letter sounds. The goal is to see how fast we can say the sounds.

I’m going to point to the letters as you say the sounds. When you get to the end, say “Blast off!” I will time you to see how fast you can say all the sounds. My turn first.

2

Going at a moderate speed, point to each letter in turn and say its sound. When you get to the bottom of the list, say “Blast off!”

PRACTICE

3

Now it’s your turn.

Call on a student to go first. Point to each letter as the student says the sound. Use a watch to time him or her and write the time on the board.

4

When each student has had a turn, repeat the process to see if students can beat their previous time.

5

If a student says the wrong letter sound, model for him or her, and have the student repeat the correct sound. When they get to “Blast off,” let them read the column again unaided.

Start with fewer than 10 different target letters, repeated several times to make 10 letters total.

Make a column longer than 10 letters that students struggle with. (Typically, these include the vowels and letters d and b.)

Word Race: Phonological Awareness Game

Goal: Given a picture of a word, the student can segment the word into individual phonemes.

In a Nutshell:

The students race to be the first one to say how many sounds are in a word, then students say the sounds in the word to earn a point.

10–15 picture cards that have two to four phonemes each.

Gather appropriate picture cards.

MODEL

1

Have students sit in a semi-circle in front of you.

We are going to have a word race today. I am going to show you a picture. To earn a point, you have to be the first one to tell me how many sounds are in the name of the picture and what the sounds are. When you know the answer, please give a thumbs up. I will call on the fastest person to give us his or her answer. If you call out the answer before I call on you, you will lose one point.

2

Show a picture of a lamp. Ask students to figure out how many phonemes are in the word and to identify the sounds.

My turn first. What is this object in the picture? How many sounds make up the word? Lamp has four sounds: /l/ /a/ /m/ /p/. The first person to get three points will be the winner of this round of Word Race. Raise your hand only when you are ready with your answer. Do not raise your hand before you are ready. The game ends when all the picture cards are used.

PRACTICE

3

Now it's your turn. Play as many rounds of the game as time allows and tally the points scored by students. If there is time, after students have identified the number of sounds in the word, ask them to think of another word that has the same number of sounds. If they can think of another word with the same number of sounds, they score an additional point.

Phoneme Identification Accuracy

Goal: Given a spoken word, the student can identify the first, middle, or last sound of the word.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Students place one chip in an Elkonin box that corresponds to a given sound’s place in the word (first, middle, or last).

Elkonin boxes (1 per student).
Chips (1 per student).
Picture cards: bed, box, fox, girl, key, kick, moon, owl, pen, pig, queen, sun.

Create an Elkonin box for each student (three connected boxes in a row, with each box representing a sound in a word). Gather appropriate picture cards and chips.

MODEL

1

Give each student one Elkonin box and one chip.

I’m going to say a word and then a sound in the word. You will figure out if the sound is the start, middle, or end of the word. Then you’ll place your chip in the first, middle, or last box.

2

Show the picture card sun.

My turn first. This is a picture of the sun, sssuuunnn. Where do you hear the /s/ sound in sun? I hear the /s/ sound at the start of the word sun, so I’m going to place my chip in the first box.

Where do you hear the /u/ sound in sun? I hear the /u/ sound in the middle of the word sun, so I’m going to place my chip in the middle box.

PRACTICE

3

Show the picture card bed.

4

Now it’s your turn. Where do you hear the /b/ sound in bed? Remember to place your chip in the right box. Call on individual students to identify where a specific phoneme occurs in a word. Vary the phoneme you are asking for: first, middle, or last. Provide support and modeling as needed.

5

Continue with the other picture cards.

Have three students stand in a line, representing the first, middle, and last phoneme in a word. When individual students point to a student in the line, that student says the respective sound. Then have the individual student identify the word. Repeat with multiple students.

Have students take turns leading the activity, asking the other students to identify where a specific phoneme occurs.

How Many Sounds? Phoneme Segmentation Accuracy

Goal: Given a spoken word, the student can segment it into separate sounds.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Students count the number of sounds in a given word and place one chip on the table for each sound.

5 chips for each student.

Gather chips. Make a list of these words for you to refer to: bake, cake, gap, get, hiss, jet, kiss, lake, lap, let, miss, nap.

MODEL

1

Give each student five chips.

Today, we are going to count the sounds in words. I am going to say a word, and you will count the number of sounds in the word. Remember, we are counting the sounds, not the letters. You will place one chip in front of you for each sound you hear.

2

My turn first. My word is: lap, laaap. There are three sounds in lap, so I am going to place three chips on the table.

PRACTICE

3

Now it’s your turn. How many sounds do you hear in miss? mmmiiisss. Remember to place one chip in front of you for each sound you hear. Call on an individual student. Then continue with the words in the list, calling on students individually. Have students put down the correct number of chips and say the phonemes in the word as they touch each chip.

4

Be sure that every student has had an individual turn to check for understanding. Model and support students as necessary.

When you give the target word, say the word very slowly so the students can hear each individual sound.

Introduce words with consonant blends, such as best, panda, plan, and stretch.

Reintroduce v

Goal: Given a printed letter, the student can say its sound.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

Students take turns saying the letter sound to erase it from the board.

  • Board to write on.

Write the letter v on the board.

MODEL

1

Point to the letter v.

Let’s review the sound for this letter. What’s the sound? Students: /v/. Good: /v/ like in the word very.

2

Write a mix of 12 letters on the board, arranged randomly. Write one v for each student in your group plus one. Intersperse those letters with other letters dissimilar in appearance to v.

3

You are going to take turns coming up to the board. You will look for the letter v, say its sound, and trace it with your finger to erase it. Remember to carefully trace over it with your finger so that only the letter disappears. My turn first: /vvv/.
Trace the letter and say the sound. As you trace the letter, it should disappear.

PRACTICE

4

Now it’s your turn.

Call on an individual student to come up to the board to say the correct sound as he or she erases the letter by tracing it.

5

If a student says the sound for one of the other letters, remind the student that he or she only needs to make the sound of the letter v and ask him or her to try again.

Write fewer distracter letters on the board. Show the students the v letter card.

Write words containing the target letter on the board. Have students find the target letter in the words.

Letter Hunt: Letter Sound Accuracy

Goal: Given printed letters, the student can discriminate between them and say the sound of each.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

After the teacher reads a story or sentence, each student finds a target letter on a page of the book and says the letter’s sound.

  • One familiar classroom book for each student, preferably the same title for each student. Make sure the target letters a, m, s, t, i, f, r, o, d, u, g, c, b, n, k, v appear on the pages of the book.
  • Board to write on.

Gather books. Write the target letters on the board: a, m, s, t, i, f, r, o, d, u, g, c, b, n, k, v.

MODEL

1

Distribute one book to each student. If necessary, pair students up to share one book.

We are going on a search for the letters a, m, s, t, i, f, r, o, d, u, g, c, b, n, k, v. After we read a page, you will look for these letters in the book. Open your book to the first page and follow along as I read.

2

Read a page of the story to the students.

3

Now you are going to look on this page for one of the letters on the board, point to it, and say its sound. My turn first.

Hold up your book, point to the letter, and say its sound.

PRACTICE

4

Now it’s your turn.

Call on each student individually to find one of the target letters on a page of his or her book and say its sound.

5

If a student cannot find a target letter, help him or her find one and ask the student to say its sound. If necessary, model the sound for the student. Then have him or her find the target letter on a different page and say the sound correctly.

If students have trouble identifying a target letter on a page, have them find the target letter in a single sentence.

Have students find all occurrences of a target letter on a page. If they are able, have them read any of the words in which the target letter appears.

Draw My Sounds: Letter Sounds Game

Goal: Given individual spoken phonemes, the student can write the phonemes, blend them, and read the word.

In a Nutshell:

The students write given phonemes, blend them, read the word, draw an image, and say a sentence using the word.

  • Draw My Sounds board (blackline master).
  • Paper, pencils, and crayons.

Generate a list of CVC words from sounds that have been covered thus far. Duplicate enough copies of the Draw My Sounds board for each student.

MODEL

1

Pass out one piece of paper and pencil to each student. Organize students into two teams.

I am going to say a sound, and I want you to write the letter for that sound in the first box on your paper.

2

Point to the correct sections on your paper as you say each sound in hat.

/h/. Write the letter for /h/ in the first box. Now write the letter for /a/ in the second box. Then in the third box, write the letter for /t/. Now let’s put all the sounds together: /h/ /a/ /t/. What is the word? Hat! In the fourth box on your paper, you will draw a hat.

Each team will have 15 seconds to use the word in a sentence. If your team can use the word in a sentence correctly, your team will earn a point. The team with the most number of points at the end wins!

3

The game ends when you have gone through all the CVC words prepared.

PRACTICE

4

Continue the activity with other CVC words.

Favorite Things: Phoneme Segmentation and Blending Accuracy

Goal: The student can segment a spoken word into separate sounds; the student can blend multiple
sounds into a word.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

The teacher gives a category of favorite things, and students say their favorite item one phoneme at a time. The group
blends the phonemes to figure out each student’s favorite thing.

None.
Generate a list of “favorite things” categories that will interest students in your group, such as games. Examples of words in this category could include tag, cards, jump rope.

MODEL

1

Choose a favorite category, such as favorite games.

Let’s talk about our favorite things. I am going to ask each of you what your favorite game is. You will answer me by saying each sound in the word. The group will then blend the sounds together to figure out your favorite thing.

2

My turn first. My favorite game is /t/ /a/ /g/. Let’s blend those sounds together: taaag, tag. What’s my favorite game? (tag)

PRACTICE

3

Call on an individual student to practice segmenting his or her favorite game into phonemes, and have the group blend the sounds together. Give each individual student a turn.

4

For students who have difficulty segmenting into phonemes, have them whisper the word to you, model segmenting it, and then have them repeat what you said. Then whisper another game for the same student to segment.

5

Continue with words from additional favorite things categories such as fruits, vegetables, etc.

Help students by whispering a simpler item for them to segment. If colors is the category, then red, for instance, is easier to segment than orange.

Have the students pick their own category and segment that portion of the sentence as well. For example: My favorite sport is sssoookerrr.

Sound Change: Phoneme Substitution Accuracy

Goal: Given a written and spoken word, the student can substitute a sound to form another word.

In a Nutshell: (5–8 minutes)

The teacher builds a CVC word using letter cards and then students change the first sound and letter card to create a different word.

Letter cards, including at least: a, b, c, f, g, l, m, n, p, r, t, v.

Gather appropriate letter cards. Prepare a list of words containing the following rime patterns for you to reference: -an, -ar, -ap. Examples include can, pan, car, far, lap, nap.

MODEL

1

Today, we are going to do an activity called Sound Change. I’m going to build a word and read it. Then you will build a new word by changing the first sound in the word I built. My turn first. Use the letter cards to build the word ran.

The first word is ran: rrraaannn, rrr-an. Point to each letter card as you say the phoneme. You may want to highlight an as a unit, so it is easier for students to separate and change the onset.

2

If I take off the first sound /r/, I have an. Now I’m going to add a different first sound to create a new word. If I add the /f/ sound at the start of an, I have fan: faaannn. Emphasize the sound change and then substitute f for r.

PRACTICE

3

Now it’s your turn. Make a new word by substituting a sound for the first sound in the word fan. What is the first sound in the word fan? That’s right: it’s /f/. What is your new word? Remember, you will change fan into that word. Your word will end with an. Call on students individually to build new words by substituting the first sound, while you substitute the appropriate letter card that represents the sound the student provides.

4

Repeat with the other rime stems. Students may form nonsense words. If they do, ask them to repeat their word and identify whether it is a real word or a nonsense word. Then have them try to form real words with a different sound. When students are comfortable with this skill, have them change the last or middle sound of the word. For instance, changing fan to fin and fin to fit.

Use different-colored chips or tokens instead of letter cards to show that one sound is being removed and a different sound is being added.

Ask students to make a new word by adding a phoneme (for example, changing ban to bang or bet to best) or deleting one (for example, changing brat to rat or sang to sag).
